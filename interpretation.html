Thesis

Initially a supporter of American expansionism, Mark Twain would eventually become a prominent anti-imperialist and vocally opposed American colonialism. These beliefs became a foundational theme for his writing.

Analysis

Twain’s early optimism about progress gave way to a sharp critique of imperialism. His essay “To the Person Sitting in Darkness” condemns U.S. colonial practices, while his letters reveal deep moral outrage. Themes of freedom and resistance in Huckleberry Finn echo this stance.

Conclusion

Twain’s transformation underscores the tension between national ambition and moral responsibility—a debate that remains relevant today.
