Thesis

Initially a supporter of American expansionism, Mark Twain would eventually become a prominent anti-imperialist and vocally opposed American colonialism. These beliefs became a foundational theme for his writing. An examination of these themes reveals a consistent moral critique of hypocrisy and injustice throughout his career.

Analysis

Twain’s early works, such as Life on the Mississippi, reflect optimism about progress and modernization. However, his later writings—especially “To the Person Sitting in Darkness”—show a dramatic shift toward condemning imperialism as a betrayal of democratic ideals. This evolution mirrors his personal struggles and growing disillusionment with authority.

In Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, Twain critiques societal hypocrisy through Huck’s moral dilemmas. This theme of questioning authority and imposed morality resonates with his later political stance against America’s imperial ambitions. His letters from the Anti-Imperialist period further confirm that his opposition was not rhetorical but deeply personal and principled.

Conclusion

Twain’s transformation from expansionist sympathizer to anti-imperialist advocate underscores the complexity of his worldview. His writings and letters offer a powerful lens to examine the tension between national ambition and moral responsibility—a debate that remains relevant today.
